# Atopic Dermatitis — Mechanism & Pathophysiology

## Overview

Atopic dermatitis (AD) is a complex inflammatory disease driven by a convergence of three interconnected pathogenic factors: immune dysregulation, skin barrier dysfunction, and abnormal neuronal sensitivity. The pathogenesis begins with genetic and environmental factors that compromise the epidermal barrier—most notably loss-of-function mutations in the filaggrin gene and alterations in ceramide composition—allowing allergen penetration. Damaged keratinocytes release alarmin cytokines including thymic stromal lymphopoietin (TSLP), IL-33, and IL-25, which activate innate lymphoid cells type 2 (ILC2s) and dendritic cells, initiating a Th2-dominated immune response. In acute-phase disease, Th2 cells and ILC2s produce IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13, which further impair barrier genes and amplify type 2 inflammation. During chronic disease, Th22 and Th17 cells become increasingly prominent, producing IL-22 which suppresses filaggrin and tight junction proteins, perpetuating barrier dysfunction.

The itch-scratch cycle is mediated by neuroimmune interactions: IL-31 produced by Th2 cells signals through IL-31 receptor A (IL-31RA) on sensory neurons expressing TRPV1 and TRPA1, activating JAK-STAT signaling that increases nerve fiber density and pruritogenic responsiveness. In parallel, dysbiosis of the skin microbiome—particularly overgrowth of pathogenic *Staphylococcus aureus* and loss of protective commensals—further compromises innate immunity through reduced antimicrobial peptide production and altered lipid metabolism. Recent mechanistic work (2024–2026) has highlighted emerging roles for costimulatory molecules (OX40/OX40L), the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) pathway in maintaining barrier and immune tolerance, and specific alterations in sphingolipid and ceramide composition as biomarkers and therapeutic targets. This multi-system model explains the failure of single-target monotherapies and supports rational combination approaches targeting barrier repair, immune modulation, and microbiota restoration.

## Key findings (2024-2026)

- **Th2/Th22/Th17 dynamics across disease phases**: Acute AD is dominated by Th2 cells producing IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13, but chronic disease involves coordinated elevation of Th22 and Th17 cells, with Th22-derived IL-22 directly suppressing filaggrin and claudin-1 expression, complicating monotherapy efficacy [Frontiers Medicine 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2024.1342176/full)

- **IL-31RA as the central neuroimmune hub**: IL-31 receptor A is expressed on a small population of TRPV1+/TRPA1+ nociceptors; IL-31 binding initiates STAT3-dependent neuronal branching and sensitization, driving the itch-scratch cycle and establishing IL-31RA antagonism (nemolizumab) as mechanistically justified antipruritic therapy [JACI 2022](https://www.jacionline.org/article/S0091-6749(22)00380-3/fulltext)

- **Mast cell–neuron axis as core mechanism (2025)**: Recent work identifies skin mast cells as a primary source of pruritogenic mediators (histamine, tryptase, nerve growth factor) that directly sensitize TRP channels and amplify neuronal firing, suggesting dual targeting of mast cells and neurons may improve pruritus control [Frontiers Immunology 2025](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/immunology/articles/10.3389/fimmu.2025.1645095/full)

- **Ceramide abnormalities as biomarkers and mechanisms**: AD skin shows reduced ultra-long-chain ceramides and increased short-chain variants (C16-ceramide, 2-hydroxy C16-ceramide); reduced protein-bound ceramides in early-life skin predict AD development, implicating ceramide composition as both a pathogenic driver and predictive biomarker [JACI 2024](https://www.jacionline.org/article/S0091-6749(24)01243-0/fulltext) and [J Dermatol 2026](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/1346-8138.70098)

- ***Staphylococcus aureus* colonization and dysbiosis mechanisms**: ~70% of lesional skin harbors *S. aureus*, producing α-toxin and δ-toxin that degrade antimicrobial peptides and eliminate protective commensals; severity inversely correlates with *Staphylococcus epidermidis* abundance. Therapeutic interventions (probiotics, phages, live biotherapeutics targeting *S. aureus*) are advancing clinically [Frontiers Cellular & Infection Microbiology 2026](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/cellular-and-infection-microbiology/articles/10.3389/fcimb.2026.1693905/full) and [Nat Microbiol 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41522-026-00953-5)

- **OX40/OX40L pathway: novel upstream target in development**: Rocatinlimab and amlitelimab (OX40/OX40L inhibitors) achieved phase 3 sustained EASI 75 responses through 52 weeks; APG279 expected to compare directly with dupilumab in phase Ib (late 2026); IMG-007 phase IIb beginning Q1 2025. OX40 acts upstream of Th2 cell activation, potentially offering improved durability [Dermatology Times 2025](https://www.dermatologytimes.com/view/ox40-ox40l-inhibitors-offer-upstream-signaling-pathways-in-atopic-dermatitis) and [BioDrugs 2025](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40259-025-00762-w)

- **Aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) in barrier maintenance and immune tolerance**: AhR activation enhances keratinocyte differentiation, increases tight junction proteins, and polarizes dendritic cells toward tolerogenic phenotypes; topical tapinarof (AhR agonist) is in phase III trials; natural product modulators of AhR signaling are being explored [Frontiers Allergy 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/allergy/articles/10.3389/falgy.2024.1323405/full) and [Molecules 2024](https://www.mdpi.com/1420-3049/29/24/5951)

- **Species-to-human translation gap in cytokine targets**: Anti-IL-33 and anti-TSLP antibodies showed limited efficacy in human trials despite central roles in murine AD models, highlighting the need for human-relevant mechanistic validation and suggesting that ILC2 crosstalk with environmental and microbial signals differs substantially between species [Frontiers Medicine 2024](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2024.1342176/full)

- **JAK/STAT-mediated repression of barrier genes under type 2 cytokine signaling**: IL-4 and IL-13 engage JAK1/STAT6, suppressing filaggrin, claudin-1, claudin-4, and tight junction assembly at the transcriptional level; this explains why JAK inhibitors (topical and systemic) improve both inflammation and barrier function [PMC 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11625510/)

- **ILC2 subset identity shaped by tissue-derived alarmins**: TSLP, IL-33, and IL-25 induce distinct ILC2 phenotypes with differential cytokine output; ILC2 survival and activation depend on coordinated signaling from TSLP and IL-2 receptor pathways, explaining variable responses to single-target ILC2-blocking therapies [Blood Advances 2017](https://ashpublications.org/bloodadvances/article/1/10/577/15619/IL-33-IL-25-and-TSLP-induce-a-distinct-phenotypic)

- **Barrier repair beyond filaggrin: multi-target lipid and tight junction strategies emerging**: Novel agents target PDE4, ceramide restoration, and claudin-1 stabilization concurrently; dupilumab (IL-4Rα blockade) also restores filaggrin and lipid synthesis, demonstrating synergy between immune suppression and barrier repair [PMC 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12304493/)

## Open questions

- Does targeting OX40/OX40L offer superior durability or reduced flare rates compared to downstream IL-4/IL-13 blockade, and can phase Ib comparisons with dupilumab demonstrate this clinically?

- What is the relative contribution of mast cell-derived versus T cell-derived IL-31 to the itch phenotype, and can selective IL-31 neutralization in one compartment provide equivalent symptom control?

- Can ceramide profiling (ultra-long-chain vs. short-chain ratios) stratify responders to barrier-repair therapies and predict need for intensified immune modulation?

- How do species-specific ILC2 responses to environmental triggers (pollen, pollutants, diet-derived aryl hydrocarbon receptor ligands) explain the translation failure of murine models, and can human organoid or patient-derived immune systems better predict efficacy?

- Is AhR activation in epithelial cells sufficient to maintain barrier function, or is concerted AhR signaling in dendritic cells and T cells essential for durable immune tolerance?

- What mechanistic insights explain why some patients with severe *Staphylococcus aureus* colonization remain controlled on anti-IL-4Rα therapy, while others flare? Are strain-specific virulence factors or commensal co-infections modulatory?

- Can live biotherapeutic or phage-based microbiota restoration approaches be combined rationally with immunosuppression to achieve synergistic disease control?

- Do circulating sphingolipid metabolites (including dihydroceramides and sphinganines) correlate with disease activity and barrier integrity across patient populations, and can they serve as non-invasive biomarkers?
